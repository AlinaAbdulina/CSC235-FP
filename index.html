Visual Analytics for Environmental Data

Who is in Paradise Pond?

An interactive exploration of upstream and downstream environmental patterns in and around Paradise Pond and the Mill River.

This project explores how Paradise Pond may shape river conditions and aquatic life. We are building an interface that helps users compare conditions before and after sediment redistribution and see how macroinvertebrate groups relate to those changes.

Our current prototype focuses on three connected views: one centered on pH, one centered on conductivity or density patterns across taxon groups, and one centered on flow and functional feeding groups.

About the Project

What are we exploring?

We are studying how sediment redistribution in Paradise Pond may be connected to changes in the Mill River ecosystem. Our project compares the two sampling sites around the pond and helps users explore how environmental variables and macroinvertebrate groups vary across time.

The dashboard is designed to support comparison, filtering, and close reading of specific groups. Users will be able to move between broad patterns and more detailed group-level views.

Why upstream and downstream?

Upstream gives us a control site, while downstream helps us look for possible effects connected to the pond and sediment redistribution.

Why these variables?

We are focusing on pH, conductivity, flow, and group-level biological patterns because these seem more informative than water temperature for this question.

What can users do?

Users will be able to compare before vs after periods, filter by season, and focus on particular taxon or feeding groups to inspect differences.

Research Context

What we have learned so far

Season matters

Summer tends to be more stable, while Fall is more variable, so season needs to stay visible in the interface.

Downstream often differs

Downstream generally shows lower pH and slightly higher flow and conductivity, along with greater overall macroinvertebrate density.

Groups respond differently

Collector filterers are often more abundant downstream, while scraper and collector gatherer patterns are more mixed and dynamic.
